## Quality issues

### Entry 117, 142, 149 (writingEdit) — three near-duplicates testing the same parallelism pattern
- **Issue:** All three test "list of bare verbs governed by a leading `to`," where the wrong third item is either a clause or `-ing` form and the right answer is the bare verb.
  - 117: "read… take… and that they answer" → "and answer"
  - 142: "stay… pack… and respecting wildlife" → "and respect wildlife"
  - 149: "admire… take… and they rested" → "and rest"
  - The structures are nearly identical (three-item list, replace last item with bare-verb form).
- **Suggested fix:** Edit one or two of these in place to test a different parallelism flavor (e.g. parallel prepositional phrases, parallel adjectives, "not only… but also" parallelism, parallel comparatives). Idx 134 already covers gerund-list parallelism, so a new variant should avoid that too.

### Entry 135 (writingEdit) — near-duplicate of entry 111 ("each" + singular verb)
- **Issue:** Both test the rule that "each of the [plural noun]" takes a singular verb. 111 is "Each of the students were asked → was asked"; 135 is "Each of the candidates have → has." Same rule, same construction, similar answer pattern.
- **Suggested fix:** Edit 135 to a different indefinite-pronoun-agreement variant — e.g. "neither of the…", "none of the… is/are", "every one of the…", or to a wholly different rule (collective noun agreement, "one of the X who/that…").

### Entry 137 (writingEdit) — near-duplicate of entry 112 (dangling modifier, fix by switching subject)
- **Issue:** Both have an introductory participial phrase followed by a passive main clause whose subject doesn't match the implied agent; the fix is to make the implied agent the subject in active voice.
  - 112: "Walking through the forest… the sound was heard by Maria" → "Maria heard…"
  - 137: "Having finished her homework… the television was turned on by Sofia" → "Sofia turned on the television"
- **Suggested fix:** Replace 137 with a dangling-modifier item that requires a different fix — e.g. rewriting the participial phrase itself ("To finish the report on time, …"), or one where the right answer is a different subject than the obvious one.

### Entry 144 (writingEdit) — near-duplicate of entry 116 (its/it's, both NO CHANGE)
- **Issue:** Both items present "its [noun]" (correct possessive) and offer the distractors "it's [noun]," "its' [noun]," and a number-mismatched pronoun, with NO CHANGE as the right answer. Identical rule, identical distractor pattern.
- **Suggested fix:** Either retire 144 to a different rule, or flip the polarity — start with "it's" (wrong) and require the student to pick "its" — so the kid actually has to recognize the contracted-vs-possessive distinction in the prompt rather than skip past it.

### Entry 78 (vocab) — synonym "sober" relies on the secondary sense
- **Passage gist:** "He was a staid figure… same gray suit, same measured speeches…"
- **Issue:** Correct is "sober." For a 12-year-old, "sober" primarily reads as "not drunk." The intended sense (serious, restrained) is the secondary meaning. The student gets the right answer by elimination, but the synonym doesn't cleanly land. Distractors (wealthy, dishonest, creative) are easy throwaways.
- **Suggested fix:** Replace "sober" with a cleaner synonym such as "sedate," "serious," or "subdued"; tighten distractors so the student must actively compare two plausible synonyms.

### Entry 14 (comprehension) — distractors too easy to eliminate
- **Passage gist:** Water cycle description (evaporation, condensation, rain, rivers…).
- **Issue:** Correct is "describe how water moves continuously through different reservoirs." All three distractors are silly (poorly understood by scientists, warn about drought, compare ocean to river evaporation). A student doesn't have to engage with the passage to answer.
- **Suggested fix:** At least one distractor should plausibly track a detail in the passage — e.g. "to argue that water molecules can outlive the species that use them" (riffs on the closing sentence) — so the student must distinguish supporting detail from main purpose.

### Entry 126 (writingEdit) — both choice B and choice C are defensible
- **Passage:** "The committee reached a decision <u>that they all agreed on it</u>…"
- **Issue:** The keyed answer is C ("they all agreed on") on conciseness grounds. Choice B ("which they all agreed on") is also grammatically correct and idiomatic — the only argument against it is the restrictive-clause preference for `that` over `which`, but with no commas surrounding the clause B reads as a reduced relative without issue. The explanation acknowledges B is "acceptable," which signals two defensible answers.
- **Suggested fix:** Either make B clearly wrong (e.g. introduce a number-mismatch or extra word that breaks it), or change the question to explicitly ask for the most concise version (and confirm C is uniquely shortest).

### Entry 58 (comprehension) — near-duplicate topic of entry 5
- **Passage gist:** Post-Yorktown America: war debts, weak government, slavery, foreign relations…
- **Issue:** Entry 5 is "framers gathered in Philadelphia… competing fears about weak vs strong government." Entry 58 covers the same window (post-Yorktown to Constitution) with the same "many problems remained unresolved" framing. Both ask a similar "main idea/period characterization" question.
- **Suggested fix:** Re-skew 58 toward a specific narrower theme of the post-revolution period (e.g. focus only on the slavery compromise, or only on foreign-debt diplomacy, or only on Shays's Rebellion) so the passages don't read as two takes on the same content.

## Style notes

### Entry 47 (comprehension) — minor scientific overstatement
- **Issue:** Passage claims octopuses "pass on no learned behavior from one generation to the next" — this is the textbook view but recent research (e.g. cumulative culture in cephalopods) has begun to chip at it. Not a question-validity problem since the correct answer flows from the passage as written, but if the bank is meant to last, a future cleanup could soften ("pass on little learned behavior" or "have no opportunity to learn from older generations").

### Entry 128 (writingEdit) — easy then/than swap with weak distractors
- **Issue:** Choices A (then), B (as), C (from), D (than). The error is the elementary then/than confusion, and B/C don't pair with "longer" so they self-eliminate immediately. Solid practice item but on the easy end for PSAT 8/9. Optional: replace B and C with more competitive comparative-construction errors (e.g. "longer that," "longer than what") to force closer reading.
